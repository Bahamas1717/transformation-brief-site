Craig Horton: The redesign has to come before the project

"We are not replacing people with AI" may be true at the level of a role, but I am seeing enterprises change the work around the role, who prepares the decision, who reviews it, what evidence remains, and who carries accountability when the output is wrong.

That is why the redesign has to come before the project.

Microsoft is changing the work

What's the latest?

This week Microsoft announced it is eliminating around 2.1% of its global workforce, about 4,800 people, as part of its company transformation.

Amy Coleman, Microsoft's Chief People Officer, said those roles are not being replaced by AI, and I believe that statement, while also believing the operating model is changing underneath it.

AI changes the volume of coordination, the decisions a manager sees first, the work that moves between teams, and the judgement leaders are expected to make when an agent is now part of the flow.

A headcount reduction without redesigned workflows is a cost action, because the same handoffs remain, the same unclear ownership remains, and the people left behind inherit more pressure with less capacity to resolve it.

A question came to mind, which work has genuinely changed, which decision has moved, who owns the outcome once an agent is involved, and where does human judgement still have authority to intervene, challenge or stop the work?

This is where many AI programmes are heading, the operating model is starting to catch up.

Why does it matter?

This week I put the same agentic HR initiative from Issue 13 back through the AI BVF.

The verdict remained Fix, leaning towards Stop, because the strategic intent was good and the workflow had a credible value case, while the gaps were still in governance and change enablement, no named owner for the agent's decisions, no defined override role, and incomplete evidence for a person affected by the outcome.

The AI BVF pre-deployment actions were clear, map each decision affecting a person, name the business owner, the control owner and the person with authority to override, design the intervention point before the agent acts, complete data lineage, model-risk documentation and the Data Protection Impact Assessment, then re-score when the evidence is strong.

The run modelled EUR 5m to EUR 9m of organisational drag across a ten-week window, and although this was illustrative, the point is real, delay costs money, while premature deployment costs trust.

The AI BVF regulatory readout from this run. Open on the MCP registry as aibvf-mcp, at github.com/Craig-Horton/ai-bvf.
The reframing question
Before you approve a headcount action attached to an AI programme, can you show the workflow that changed, the decisions that moved, and the named person who owns the outcome?
